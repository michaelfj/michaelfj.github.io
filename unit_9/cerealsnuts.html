Cereal grains

Cooked cereal flours can still be a problem. The T-2 toxins of Fusarium mould are so heat stable that they can easily survive baking in bread. T-2 toxin has been used as a biological warfare agent since it was discovered in Russia in the 1950s after it killed many people who ate bread baked with contaminated flour. Chlorine bleaching of flour kills this mould so white bread may actually be safer than brown or wholemeal! Fusarium mould has been found in surveys to contaminate 90% of wheat and 70% of oats.

Would you expect the raw oats in muesli to be a problem? Well yes. The lightly-cooked wheat in (organic) wheat biscuits? Quite possibly. Organic alternative cereals for the gluten intolerant can be a big problem - quinoa and amaranth are imported from poor third world countries.

Tip

It is OK to eat cereal products but they should be kept in the freezer then rinsed and cooked well. Any dust from the cereal packs need to be cleaned up off your hands and the kitchen surfaces.

Nuts

Nuts are now very popular as low sugar, low GI snack.
Qu: Is opening a packet of nuts and putting your hand in dangerous?
A: Yes. Roasted nuts are a safer option than raw but could still be a problem.
Qu: Could ground-up nut products be a problem - peanut butter, almond meal, cashew paste, chocolate (ground cocoa nut)?
A: Yes. It has been reported that up to 70% of peanut butters are contaminated with Aflatoxins from Aspergillus mould..

Your environment

Toxic mould will inhabit warm, moist areas in your house including the drain below the kitchen sink. In one study the toxic Fusarium mould was found in 66% of sink pipes. What are the most likely places for mould:

  • Wet areas - batrooms and laundries
  • Bedroom ceilings
  • Kitchen sinks and cupboards
  • Clothing and shoe cupboards
  • Verandah posts and window sills
  • Air-conditioner filters
  • Underhouse storage areas.
